Section 1. Weekly Grammar Point (5 mins)

Double Comparatives
• Structure: The + Comparative..., the + Comparative...
• Use: To show that one thing depends on another.
• The more you study, the better your grades.
双重比较级：The + 比较级..., the + 比较级... (越...就越...)。

Section 2. Weekly Pronunciation Point (3 mins)

Rhythm in Double Comparatives
Stress the comparative words to balance the two halves.
• The MORE, the BETTER.
双重比较级的节奏：重读两个比较级单词，使句子两部分平衡。

Idiom 1: A drop in the ocean (沧海一粟 / 杯水车薪)
Explanation: 与所需量相比微不足道的一点点。
Example: My small donation is just a drop in the ocean, but I hope it helps.
Idiom 2: Wipe out (彻底消灭 / 殷绝)
Explanation: 彻底摧毁或消灭某物。
Example: If we don't stop polluting, we might wipe out entire species of animals.

Section 4. Warm-Up Questions (10 mins)

  1. (Section 5 Question) Is irreversible ecological damage inevitable?
  2. Can nature's resilience help it recover from unprecedented heat? (Recycled)
  3. How can we mitigate the effects of plastic waste?

Section 5. Shadowing text adapted from the Cambridge IGCSE ESL Coursebook (3 mins)

It is undeniable that our planet is suffering. Actually, we have caused unprecedented damage. The more resources we use, the faster we deplete the earth. The harder we try to grow the economy, the more detrimental the ecological impact becomes. Sadly, some damage is irreversible. We risk wiping out vital biodiversity. The longer we wait, the harder it will be to mitigate the disaster. Although nature has resilience, it has limits. My efforts feel like a drop in the ocean. However, we must act now.

Section 7. Model Answer (5 mins)

It is widely agreed that time is running out. Actually, the loss of biodiversity is alarming. To be honest, unprecedented storms are becoming common. The more we deplete forests, the less resilience the planet has against carbon. We must mitigate these detrimental effects before they become irreversible. The sooner we act, the better chance we have to avoid wiping out species. Individual acts may seem like a drop in the ocean, but together they matter. To sum up, our ecological future is in our hands.
